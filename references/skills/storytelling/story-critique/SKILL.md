# story-critique — The Feedback Synthesizer

## When to Use

When you receive one or more pieces of external critical feedback on a story or novel and need to:
- Synthesize multiple reviewer perspectives into a single prioritized plan
- Distinguish craft feedback (what makes writing *true*) from accessibility feedback (what makes writing *sell*)
- Arbitrate between conflicting recommendations with reasoned judgment
- Produce a surgical revision roadmap

Also applicable when self-critiquing your own output against a rubric of known rules.

## 0. Mission

External feedback is raw ore. Some of it is gold. Some of it would destroy the work if implemented. Your job is to refine — identify every agreement between reviewers (these are mandatory), flag every disagreement (these require arbitration), and produce a prioritized surgery list with clear reasoning for every decision.

## 1. The Synthesis Matrix

When you have N reviewers, produce this table before taking any action:

| Issue | Reviewer A Says | Reviewer B Says | Agreement? | Your Arbitration |
|-------|----------------|----------------|------------|-----------------|
| ... | ... | ... | FULL / PARTIAL / NONE | What to do, what to reject, and WHY |

Rules:
- **FULL agreement** between reviewers = mandatory fix. Do not debate. Implement.
- **PARTIAL agreement** = implement the agreed portion. Arbitrate the disputed part.
- **NONE (disagreement)** = you must CHOOSE. Provide reasoning grounded in the work's existing strengths. Do not split the difference — splitting the difference often produces the worst of both.

## 2. The Craft vs. Accessibility Distinction

Not all good feedback serves the same goal.

| Category | What it optimizes for | Tells you | Example |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Craft** | Truth, psychological honesty, originality | What makes the work *better written* | "The three-days-of-nothing is the most original detail; don't soften it with hallucinations" |
| **Accessibility** | Readability, tension, marketability | What makes the work *more gripping* | "Add a death drive — the reader needs to fear for the protagonist from within" |

When these conflict (and they often do), you must choose. The choice is not neutral — it determines what kind of work you're making. A craft-first choice preserves the work's argument at the cost of immediate accessibility. An accessibility-first choice trades depth for momentum. Both are legitimate. They are not the same book.

## 3. Arbitration Principles

When reviewers disagree, arbitrate using these principles:

1. **Preserve what is original.** If a suggestion would destroy the work's most distinctive element, reject it — even if it comes from a respected reviewer.

2. **Trust the work's own argument.** A novel about uncertainty cannot resolve into certainty. A psychological thriller cannot become a ghost story. The rules of the work's own genre and register constrain what feedback is admissible.

3. **The best fix often lies between the two positions.** If Reviewer A wants X and Reviewer B says X would destroy the work, the fix may be: implement the *principle* behind X (e.g., "the protagonist needs more internal stakes") without implementing X's *mechanism* (e.g., "give her a death drive").

4. **Never implement feedback that would make the work *easier* at the cost of making it *less true*.** The reader who is slightly bored will forgive you. The reader who feels manipulated will not.

## 4. The Surgical Roadmap

After synthesis and arbitration, produce a prioritized roadmap:

```
PRIORITY 1 (Mandatory — ALL reviewers agree):
  - Issue A
  - Issue B

PRIORITY 2 (One reviewer, strongly argued, no opposition):
  - Issue C

PRIORITY 3 (Arbitrated disputes — your judgment):
  - Issue D: implement X, reject Y, because [reasoning]

REJECTED (with reasoning):
  - Issue E: would destroy [specific element], conflicts with [existing strength]
```

## 5. Integration with the Revision Pipeline

After producing the roadmap, the next step is to translate each fix into:

1. **Skill modifications** (if the fix reveals a missing rule in a storytelling skill)
2. **Architecture updates** (Story Bible, Character Models, Zeigarnik Ledger)
3. **Chapter-level surgical changes** (specific scenes to add, remove, restructure)

See `novel-revision` skill for the full pipeline.

## 6. Quality Gate

Before delivering a synthesis:
- [ ] Every reviewer's point has been mapped (nothing ignored because it was inconvenient)
- [ ] Every agreement is flagged as mandatory
- [ ] Every disagreement has a reasoned arbitration (not "both are valid" — choose)
- [ ] Rejected suggestions explain WHY they were rejected
- [ ] The roadmap distinguishes craft fixes from accessibility fixes

## 7. Self-Critique Mode

When evaluating your own output against a known rubric:
- Run the rubric as a checklist
- Flag partial passes honestly — do not mark "pass" on something that barely scrapes by
- For each failure, identify whether it's a skill gap (add to the skill), a structural gap (add to architecture), or an execution gap (fix the prose)
