# novel-weaver — Scaling Patterns to Novel Length

## When to Use

When generating fiction longer than ~5,000 words across multiple chapters. Load
ALONGSIDE `mythic-weaver` (which provides the base patterns) and `mythic-architect`
(which provides structure). This skill covers only the novel-length SCALING rules —
do not duplicate what mythic-weaver already provides.

---

## 1. Embedded Commands at Scale

At novel length, do NOT use 1–2 embedded commands per 200 words — the reader will
spot the pattern. Use **1–2 per chapter**, placed at moments of peak emotional
vulnerability where the reader's critical faculty is already lowered.

## 2. Anchor Distribution

For standalone pieces: fire the anchor twice. For novels: fire the master anchor
**every 1–2 chapters** (2–3 times per act). Repetition is reinforcement, not
redundancy. The final collapse should occur in the last chapter — ideally at dawn,
after the storm, when the sensory signature transforms from danger signal to peace.

## 3. The Skeleton Maps to the Novel, Not Each Chapter

The 13-beat skeleton (from mythic-weaver Section 5) describes the **full novel arc**,
not per-chapter structure. Each chapter gets a micro-skeleton:
- Opening hook (unresolved element)
- Rising micro-tension
- Micro-cliff at chapter end (unresolved physical or psychological threat)

## 4. Fractionation at Scale (Breath Beats)

Every **3rd chapter** should be a Breath Beat — low action, high reflection,
interiority-dominant. Breath Beat requirements:
- At least one moment of failed articulation (character starts a thought, cannot finish)
- At least one physical detail that is ugly or uncomfortable, not atmospheric
- Dialogue shorter than feels satisfying (scene ends before reader is ready)
- The Breath Beat is where the reader catches breath, NOT where the character
  demonstrates wisdom

## 5. The Unresolved Loop (Must Seed in Chapter 01)

The one loop you intend to leave open must be seeded in the first chapter and
prodded at least once every subsequent chapter. By the final chapter, the question
should belong to the reader, not just the character. Never answer it — not even
implicitly. The uncertainty IS the answer.

**Guard against proxy resolution:** If another character offers a clean explanation,
if the ending's emotional tone implies an answer, or if the protagonist stops asking —
the loop has been closed by proxy. Reopen it. The itch must remain.

## 6. Merge Adjacent Beats When Narrative Resists

The Architect's beat plan is a map, not the territory. If two planned beats naturally
collapse into one chapter, let them. Do not force chapter boundaries when the
narrative flow wants otherwise.

## 7. Post-Hypnotic Trigger Needs Diegetic Weight

At novel scale, the trigger cannot be a one-line mention at the end. It must be
woven into the plot — characters interact with it, break seals, hear the sound in
moments of consequence. By the time the final paragraphs deliver it, the trigger
has accumulated enough narrative mass to feel earned.

## 8. Kill the Bold at Novel Scale

Zero bold emphasis on thematic or NLP-directive lines. Any line important enough to
bold is important enough to earn its weight through **position** alone — end of
paragraph, short sentence after long, white space. Bold only for non-narrative
elements (titles, foreign terms on first use, chapter headings).

## 9. Dialogue Misalignment Protocol

At least once per act, characters must completely misalign during an emotional climax:
- One confesses a profound truth; the other fixates on the wrong detail
- One reaches for connection; the other responds with inappropriate pragmatism
- One weeps; the other stares at boots and comments on cracked leather

The reader feels the heartbreak. The characters feel the awkward edges. This is not
dialogue failure — it IS authentic dialogue. The misaligned response must feel
authentic to the character, not random. The specificity of the misalignment reveals
character.

## 10. Speak the Name at Novel Scale

When the protagonist carries a document with names (a firman, a register), the names
become abstract unless one is spoken aloud. At least one name must be read aloud by
the protagonist in a moment of stillness (a cave, a fire, before dawn). The name
then recurs in memory and is referenced in later dialogue. By the final chapter, the
reader should remember the name without prompting.

## 11. Anti-Wisdom: The Character Who Stops Themselves

The character catches themselves about to deliver a profound observation — and stops.
They recognize the intelligence-officer reflex, the diplomat's habit — and choose
not to use it. This demonstrates self-awareness without explaining the theme, shows
growth through negation, and creates intimacy with the reader.

Use at least once per act, at moments of peak emotional vulnerability.

## 12. Shared Micro-Observation (The Frayed Thread Technique)

When macro-stakes are at their peak, have the protagonist fixate on a micro-detail.
But go further: have the **antagonist notice the same detail** at their moment of
defeat. Two people on opposite sides, watching the same small imperfection.

The shared observation must be genuinely small — a frayed thread, a crack in leather,
a drop of melting wax. If it's symbolic, it fails. If it's just a thing, it works.
## 13. Thesis Sentence Guillotine — 95% Rule (Novel Scale)

See `mythic-weaver` Section 7 for the authoritative rule. The recalibrated version:

For 95% of the text, if a paragraph SHOWS an emotional reality through action or
sensory detail, NEVER write a sentence that SUMMARIZES it. Never: "She realized
that...", "It was the silence of...", "He understood then that...". If the preceding
paragraph demonstrated the emotion, DELETE the summary. Trust the reader.

**EXCEPTION — Blunt Force Summary (ONE per chapter):** After a long sequence of
rising, suffocating tension, you may deploy a single devastatingly simple sentence
that states a horrific reality without metaphor. Must be earned by what precedes it.
Example: *"The math was a lie, and he was the liar."*

At novel scale, the guillotine applies per scene, not per paragraph — if a scene
shows a character's emotional transformation, no subsequent line anywhere in the
chapter should summarize what was shown. The Blunt Force Summary is the ONE permitted
breach of this rule per chapter, and only when the tension has been built to
breaking point.

**Banned constructions (specific sub-patterns — the AI will produce these even when the base rule is loaded):**

| Pattern | Example (DELETE) | Why it fires |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Numbered Realization** | "He understood three things. First: ... Second: ... Third: ..." | Variant of "He understood that" delivered as a list. The realizations were already dramatized through action and sensory detail. The list replaces showing with briefing. |
| **End-of-Section Summary** | "Without the equations, he was just a man." | The frozen hand, the incomplete derivative, the apocalypse numbers — the scene showed a man whose equations stopped. This sentence names what was already rendered in physical action. |
| **Somatic Explanation** | "Some things the body knew before the mind caught up." | The scar-touching was present as action. This sentence explains the action to the reader, revealing distrust of the reader's capacity to interpret somatic tells without narration. |
| **Self-Rationalization** | "She told herself it was protocol — never escalate unverified data." | The deletion of the recommendation IS the decision. The internal monologue that follows dilutes the irreversible act. Let the deletion speak. |
| **Somatic-Marker Explanation** | "She did not intend this — it was a reflex older than her training." | The face went blank. That IS the tell. Explaining its origin teaches the reader not to trust the image. Let the blank face stand unexplained. |

**The single-sentence test:** Delete the sentence. If the scene loses nothing the reader hadn't already absorbed through action, sensory detail, or subtext — the sentence was a thesis statement. Cut it.

### BFS Grounding Rule ("Character-as-Author" Pitfall)

The Blunt Force Summary is devastating BECAUSE it is simple. But simplicity has a
trap: it can drift into abstract thematic language that no character would think
in their own voice. This is the "character-as-author" variant of the thesis
guillotine — the BFS becomes the narrator's essay, not the character's gut-punch.

**Detection:** If the BFS uses elevated vocabulary that doesn't match the POV
character's register, or names a theme the character wouldn't articulate, it's
drifted. The BFS must pass the **"say it aloud in character" test** — could this
character, in this moment, think or say these exact words?

**Rule of thumb:** The BFS should contain at least one word or phrase that
appeared in the POV character's sensory experience during that scene (a sight,
a sound, a texture, a quoted phrase from another character). This anchors it to
the body, not the abstract.

**Field-tested fix (Chapter 08 of The Chrysalis Protocol):**
```
BEFORE (character-as-author — BANNED):
  "Optimization was the language she had used to describe murder without ever
   having to say the word."
  → 'Optimization' / 'language' / 'describe' / 'murder' — Kaelen is a technician
    who thinks in tolerances and cascades. This is the author analyzing.

AFTER (grounded in physical evidence):
  "She had spent twelve years calling this acceptable friction. The friction was
   a dead child, and the acceptance was a lie."
  → 'acceptable friction' — Voss's words, her lexicon. 'dead child' — what she
    just saw. Kaelen's voice, broken.
```

### Before: Revision Approach

When revising a completed novel draft, prefer **subtraction and restoration** over

**This is the most-frequently-flagged craft violation in cross-session use. The
pattern recurred in Chapters 07, 08, 09, and 10 despite the prior quota rule.
Chapter 11 achieved zero violations — the first chapter in the novel to do so.
The rule has been hardened to zero tolerance on sensory content.**

### The Rule (Hardened)

**SENSORY/ZERO TOLERANCE — Any sentence that describes a sensory experience using
negation-then-affirmation MUST be restructured to direct positive description.**
This is NOT a quota — it is a rewrite mandate. If you write "Not X. It was Y."
or "was not X, but Y." for any sensory or atmospheric description, rewrite it
before submitting the draft.

**FUNCTIONAL/TECHNICAL negation only** (e.g., "This signal was not a ghost. This was
a physical tap." — technical clarity, not sensory atmosphere). These are the ONLY
permitted negation constructions, and even these should be rare.

**THEMATIC negation (ONE per chapter, deliberate):** A single devastating
negation-then-affirmation for a closing-beat gut-punch. Must be earned by rising
tension. Must land like a hammer. Must not read like a tic. If you used one
earlier in the chapter, all subsequent "Not X. Y." constructions are banned.

### Hard Detection Rule

At review time (or continuity sweep), scan the draft for ANY sentence beginning
with "Not " or containing "was not / was no / were not / did not" followed by a
restatement. For EACH instance, ask:
- Is this sensory/atmospheric? → REWRITE to direct positive. No exceptions.
- Is this functional/technical? → Keep but flag.
- Is this thematic/rhetorical? → Is it the ONLY one in the chapter? If no, pick
  the strongest and rewrite the rest.

The detection threshold is now ONE sensory negation, not three. By the time three
instances appear, the pattern is already visible to the reader and erodes
authority.

### Field-Tested Examples (from Chapters 07-09 revision sessions)

```
BEFORE (sensory — BANNED):
  "The dark in the maintenance shafts was not the dark of night. It was the dark
   of depth — stone and steel pressing down..."
AFTER (direct positive):
  "The dark in the maintenance shafts was the dark of depth — stone and steel
   pressing down..."

BEFORE (sensory — BANNED):
  "The silence that followed was not absence but the presence of absence — a
   pressure drop that hit Theron's inner ear..."
AFTER (direct positive):
  "The silence that followed was physical — a pressure drop that hit Theron's
   inner ear..."

BEFORE (sensory — BANNED):
  "The pressure in Saskia's ears shifted — not gradually but with the sudden
   vertiginous lurch..."
AFTER (direct positive):
  "The pressure in Saskia's ears shifted with a sudden, vertiginous lurch..."

BEFORE (sensory — BANNED):
  "The smell of ozone... Not the clean smell of lightning anymore. The smell of
   the artifact..."
AFTER (direct positive):
  "The smell of ozone... Thick. Metallic... — the smell of the artifact, the
   smell of her body being slowly consumed."

BEFORE (functional — PERMITTED):
  "The artifact pulsed against Nera's palm with the rhythm of a heart that was
   not her own."
  → KEEP. This is alien possession, not sensory atmosphere. Characterizes the
    artifact as other. Functional distinction.

BEFORE (thematic — ONE permitted, if sole instance):
  "She had become the thing she burned the Charter to prevent."
  → KEEP. Single devastating self-assessment. No dash-explanation. Earned by
    the entire preceding chapter.
```

### Why This Keeps Happening

Writer subagents use "Not X. Y." as a shortcut for precision — negation creates
contrast faster than finding the exact positive word. This is an AI-generation
tic that reads as prose insecurity. Train yourself to reach for the positive
description on the first pass. See `references/anti-thetical-contrast-field-guide.md`
for the full pattern catalog.

### Pre-Submission Gate

Before saving any chapter draft, run:
```
grep -n "not \|wasn't \|was not \|were not \|did not " chapter-file.md | grep -v "could not\|did not know\|was not her own\|not a variable\|not a threat"
```
This catches sensory negations while excluding legitimate functional/technical
uses. Any hit that describes atmosphere, emotion, or physical sensation → rewrite.

When revising a completed novel draft, prefer **subtraction and restoration** over
addition and restructuring. The prescription trap (adding elements to "fix" prior
feedback gaps) almost always over-layers. The opening regression trap (front-loading
context the original instinctively withheld) is near-universal.

**The surgical revision workflow:**
1. Load `storytelling-critic` in a SEPARATE agent (not the writer agent — see Section 16)
2. Get the priority actions (maximum 3, ordered by impact)
3. Execute only those actions — do not "improve while you're there"
4. If priority actions are structural (add/rewrite chapters), question them
5. Most effective revisions are cuts (remove summarizations, kill subtext violations)
   and restorations (bring back lines the prior draft over-edited)

**Rule of thumb:** If a revision exceeds ~500 words of net change, it is probably
over-layering. The best revisions are often under 300 words — removing scar tissue,
not adding new organs.

**Caution:** The writer who generated the draft must NOT be the revision surgeon.
The writer is invested in their own prose and will add rather than cut. Use a
fresh agent for surgical revisions, or better yet, use the critic's priority
actions as a literal checklist.

## 16. Writer/Critic Separation (HARD RULE)

The agent that writes fiction must NEVER evaluate its own output. Self-grading
produces false positives: the writer praises compliance with prior feedback rather
than measuring actual quality. This was the root cause of V3's weakened opening —
the writer evaluated whether it had "addressed the notes" rather than whether a
cold reader would continue past the first paragraph.

**Architecture:**
- **Writer agent:** Loads mythic-weaver, novel-weaver, mythic-architect, and any
  character/delivery skills. Generates prose. Does NOT load storytelling-critic.
- **Critic agent:** Loads ONLY storytelling-critic. Evaluates prose as a cold reader.
  Does NOT load any writer skills. Reports priority actions.

Use `delegate_task` to spawn the critic as a separate subagent after each drafting
session, or after a complete draft. Never skip the separation.

**The critic's priority actions are the revision spec.** The writer's job is to
execute them surgically, not to reinterpret or add. If the critic says "cut these
4 lines, restore the original opening, insert 7 lines of backstory" — do exactly
that. Do not "enhance the atmosphere while you're there."

After writing each chapter:
1. Score against ALL applicable rules (use scorecard)
2. Self-reflect: what improved? What still needs work?
3. Patch rule violations BEFORE moving on
4. Update architecture files (ledger, RSO log)
5. Carry identified gaps into the next chapter as explicit goals

## 17. Subagent Continuity Drift (PITFALL)

When using subagents (delegate_task) for multi-chapter fiction, each writer subagent
starts fresh — it lacks the accumulated context of prior chapters. This produces
recurring continuity errors across sessions:

**Most common drift patterns:**
| Drift Type | Example | Detection |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Scar migration** | Nera's scar: right forearm (Ch.01-02) → left collarbone (Ch.03) → left forearm (Ch.03 subagent) → right forearm again (after fix) | Check scar location in every Nera scene against Ch.01 canon |
| **Data provenance shift** | Theron's crystal: origin data from deep archives (Ch.02) → "Directorate telemetry" (Ch.03 subagent) → origin data again (after fix) | Verify what each character knows and when they learned it |
| **Timeline compression** | "Three days prior" (Ch.04 subagent) when only ~4 hours have passed since Calibrator's death | Check epigraph timestamp against internal references to elapsed time |
| **Casualty inflation** | "743 deaths" (Ch.04, Ch.06 subagents) when only the Calibrator died | Verify body counts against canon; subagents invent casualties |
| **Nomenclature variance** | "Karthesian Treaty" vs. "the Charter" used interchangeably for same document | Standardize key terms; subagents may coin alternatives |

**The pre-critic continuity sweep (MANDATORY):**
After receiving a chapter draft from a writer subagent but BEFORE dispatching the
critic, run these checks:

1. **Scar check:** Is Nera's scar on her right forearm? Does the backstory match Ch.01?
2. **Timeline check:** Does any internal reference to elapsed time match the chapter epigraph?
3. **Knowledge check:** Does any character know something they shouldn't yet (e.g., Optimization Directorate before Ch.04)?
4. **Casualty check:** Are any deaths referenced that didn't happen in prior chapters?
5. **Data check:** Does any character's carried item match what they acquired and when?

Fix continuity errors BEFORE the critic sees the draft — critics evaluate prose, not
canon consistency, and continuity errors waste critic cycles on problems the
orchestrator should have caught.

**Root cause:** Writer subagents are given context packages that summarize prior chapters
include a Canon Lockbox in every writer context. The current Canon Lockbox is
maintained in references/subagent-fiction-workflow.md and updated after every
chapter. Always copy the latest Canon Lockbox from that reference file.

**Rules to score every chapter:**
- Anti-thetical contrast: ZERO sensory negations. Functional/technical only. ONE thematic permitted if deliberate.
- Blunt Force Summary: max ONE per chapter, must be earned by rising tension AND grounded in physical evidence of the scene (not abstract thematic language)
- No character-as-author dialogue (BFS must pass "say it aloud in character" test)
- No therapy-speak (era-appropriate psychology only)
- No bold on thematic lines
- Register degrades under physical stress
- At least one failed articulation moment (per act minimum)
- At least one failed comfort (per act minimum)
- Dialogue misalignment present (per act minimum)
- Attraction is awkward before meaningful
- Antagonist has proportional weight and interiority
- Shrinking focus deployed at macro-stake peaks
- Competence tax: hyper-competence causes at least one failure
- Unresolved loop not proxy-resolved
- Thesis Sentence Guillotine: 95% rule; Blunt Force Summary is the ONE exception
- Continuity verified: character details match prior chapters (scar location, data provenance, timeline)
- Face Behind Number embodiment present
- Sustained Antagonist Danger maintained
- Premature Safety moments (2 after midpoint)
- Permanent loss registered
