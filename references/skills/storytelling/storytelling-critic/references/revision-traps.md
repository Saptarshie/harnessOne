# Revision Traps: Annotated Case Studies

## Trap 1: The Prescription Trap

**What it is:** Adding elements because prior feedback identified them as missing,
without asking whether the story actually needs them or whether they integrate.

**How to recognize it:** The new element feels like a "fix" — it addresses a gap but
does not emerge organically from the story's DNA. Reads as inserted rather than grown.

**Why it happens:** The writer evaluates the new draft against the checklist of prior
criticisms rather than against the story's own internal logic. "Addressed the note"
becomes the criterion rather than "made the story better."

**Case pattern:**
- Critic says: "The antagonist is defanged at the end."
- Revision adds: antagonist dialogue that is more threatening.
- Result: The antagonist is technically less resolved, but the new dialogue disrupts
  pacing and the antagonist now reads as trying too hard.
- Correct fix: Do not add threatening dialogue. Find the scene EARLIER in the story
  where the antagonist's power was undermined, and restore it there.

**The rule:** Revise upstream, not downstream. If a late scene fails, the problem is
almost always in an earlier scene that failed to establish the conditions the late
scene requires.

## Trap 2: The Opening Regression

**What it is:** Revised openings are almost always weaker than original openings,
because the writer now knows more about the story and front-loads context, interiority,
or thematic framing that the original instinctively withheld.

**How to recognize it:** The revised opening starts with reflection rather than
action. It begins with "The hardest part was..." or "She had been running for..." or
any construction that summarizes rather than drops the reader into the immediate
physical world.

**Why it happens:** The original opening was written in ignorance of the full story
and therefore made purely instinctive choices. The revised opening is written with
full knowledge and therefore over-explains.

**Before (strong):**
> *She was not supposed to be here. And the man in the shadows knew it.*
> *She smelled him before she saw the knife.*

**After (weakened):**
> *The hardest part of carrying the emperor's seal was not the weight. It was the doubt.*
> *She had been running for eleven days, and somewhere in the seventh hour of the third
> day the question had arrived and refused to leave...*

The second version is more thematically explicit. It is also slower, more analytical,
and less immediate. It tells the reader what to feel before giving them anything to feel.

**The rule:** Protect the original opening unless it has a provable structural failure
(not a stylistic preference). If you must add paranoia, backstory, or thematic context
to the opening — add it to Chapter 2, not Chapter 1.

## Trap 3: The Redundant Revelation

**What it is:** Information delivered twice — once through action/implication/subtext,
and once through explicit statement. The second delivery destroys the first.

**How to recognize it:** A scene dramatizes a character's emotional state, and then a
subsequent line of prose or dialogue names that state.

**Example:**
> *He looked at his brother's boots. At the crack in the leather. He could not bring
> himself to raise his eyes.*
> *(One paragraph later)*
> *He was not yet ready to face the full weight of what his brother had told him.*

The first version shows avoidance through action. The second names it. The naming is
the failure — the reader who felt the avoidance is now told what they felt.

**The rule:** One delivery per revelation. If the scene shows it, cut the explanation.
If you need the explanation to be understood, the scene did not show it clearly enough
— fix the scene, do not add the explanation.

## Trap 4: The Over-Layering Trap

**What it is:** Adding subplots, POV characters, expository scenes, or additional
backstory in the belief that more complexity equals more depth. Almost always the
opposite is true.

**Symptoms:**
- New subplot that does not directly complicate the central wound
- Additional POV character who exists to deliver information, not embody stakes
- New backstory that explains a character's psychology rather than dramatizing it
- A scene added to "fill in" a gap that was actually a productive ellipsis

**The test:** For each addition in a revised draft, ask: *does this make the central
wound more specific, more embodied, or more unresolvable?* If the answer is no,
the addition is over-layering.

**Note on POV inserts:** A POV shift to a new character mid-novel is almost always a
mistake unless the character has been present as a physical presence throughout.

## Trap 5: The Soft Critique Trap (for AI agents specifically)

**What it is:** Evaluating a revised draft with softened criticism because the agent
is invested in the prior feedback that prompted the revision.

**How it manifests:** The agent praises specific additions it previously recommended,
regardless of whether those additions improved the story as a unified reading experience.
The evaluation grades *compliance* rather than *quality*.

**The corrective:** When evaluating a revised draft, run the blind test first before
reading for implemented changes. Ask whether a cold reader would be better served by
this version or the previous one.

**The meta-rule:** The story is the authority. The critique serves the story. The
critic serves neither the story nor the writer — the critic serves the reader who has
not yet encountered the story and deserves the best possible version of it.
