# Wound & Stakes: Extended Framework

## What Makes a Wound Specific

A wound is specific when it cannot be extracted from the character who carries it and transplanted into a different story. Test: if you can describe the wound in a sentence that could appear in a dozen other novels, it is generic.

**Generic:** "She carries guilt over something she did in the past."
**Specific:** "She does not know whether she made a mistake or a choice, and the uncertainty is worse than guilt would be, because guilt would at least confirm she is the kind of person who feels things."

The specificity is in the *form* of the wound, not just its content.

## The Three Registers of Stakes

### Register 1: Survival Stakes
What threatens physical existence or external position. Necessary for genre fiction to function but never sufficient for memorable fiction. Create urgency, not meaning.

### Register 2: Relational Stakes
What threatens bonds to specific other people. Creates emotional investment. Where most commercial fiction lives.

### Register 3: Moral Stakes
What the protagonist has to become — or discover they already are — to survive or succeed. The only register that can produce genuinely unforgettable fiction, because it puts the reader's own self-concept at risk.

## The Embodiment Principle

Abstract stakes must always be grounded in one specific, named, humanized instance. The instance does not have to be important to the plot. It has to be *seen*.

A child's wooden toy left in the mud after a cavalry raid is not plot-relevant. It is stakes-embodiment. The reader now knows what sixteen thousand names means.

Rules for embodiment:
- One specific name, with one specific detail (age, profession, a single physical object)
- The detail must be *earned* — the reader must encounter it BEFORE the stakes are invoked, so invocation is a return, not an introduction
- The detail must be small enough to be fully imagined — a wooden horse, not a family history

## The Wound and the Ending

The most common failure in endings is domestication of the wound — the story arranges for the character to achieve a new equilibrium that makes the wound bearable or resolved. This satisfies the reader and evacuates the story of its lasting power.

The alternative: an ending that changes the character's *relationship* to the wound without healing it. The wound remains. What has changed is whether the character is running from it or carrying it.

**Domesticated ending:** Character forgives themselves, is forgiven, or arrives at a clean moral position regarding their wound.

**Honest ending:** Character chooses to keep going with full knowledge of what they are and what they have done, without the comfort of resolution. The wound does not close.

The reader leaves not with the pleasure of completion but with the particular unease of recognition — *I know this. I have felt this. I do not know what I would do.*
